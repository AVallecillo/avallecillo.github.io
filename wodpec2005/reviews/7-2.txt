4) JUSTIFICATION AND GENERAL COMMENTS:

The authors' observations, considerations, reasoning, and proposals are 
interesting and certainly deserve further discussion. At the same time, not all of 
them appear to be appropriate or even correct, see below.


The most important proposal, that of the need to "add proper explanations" 
(Section 5), is very useful and relates to the need to update the non-normative 
Part 1 of RM-ODP. 


It is doubtful that "[a] solid background in formal methods is indeed 
required to make sense of many definitions or important notes in the standard.". A 
background in mathematics (defined as the art and science of effective 
reasoning) and exact philosophy does indeed help (see, for example, Bunge's 
_Philosophical Dictionary_). 


While the term "model" may have different meanings in the ODP and UML 
communities, this is not a shortcoming per se: firstly, there are various other 
communities, and UML should not be more equal than others in this context; 
secondly, as we know from RM-ODP, a name makes sense only in its context; and thirdly, 
model(l)ing is described in RM-ODP 2-6 with reference to the universe of 
discourse being model(l)ed. The paper under review does not mention these 
considerations, referring instead to UML. It is rather well-known that terms not 
defined in RM-ODP have been considered to have the definitions from the Oxford 
English Dictionary, and its definition of a model is as follows: "A simplified or 
idealized description or conception of a particular system, situation, or 
process (often in mathematical terms: so mathematical model) that is put forward 
as a basis for calculations, predictions, or further investigation". Therefore 
the authors' reasoning related to the definition of a model appears to be 
faulty.


With respect to communication and observability, the authors appear to miss 
important aspects of RM-ODP Part 2. Specifically, the statement that "[s]ince 
the behaviour of implicit communication links is well understood and 
anticipated by a specifier, these objects should perhaps not count as observers" appears 
to be doubtful: nothing in Part 2 suggests that observers cannot be 
well-understood. The following statements about observability in 2.2 also appear to be 
doubtful.


With respect to composition, it is doubtful that all the actions of the 
component objects are also actions of the composite since composition is a form of 
abstraction, and some or all of the actions of the component objects may well 
be suppressed in this abstraction (see also Part 2-6). The need for 
abstraction in model(l)ing is justified precisely by the ability to suppress irrelevant 
details (using levels and viewpoints) thus enhancing understanding by humans. 
If "interface references are not communicated outside of the composite" then 
this may be a good realization of this suppression.


The authors' proposal in 5.1 also seems to ignore the need for abstraction: 
various objects (and actions) may be suppressed at various abstraction levels 
and viewpoints, and so the fragment "all the objects" does not help in 
understanding (especially taking into account that at different abstraction levels the 
same object may be atomic or composite with different decompositions). 
Furthermore, the concepts of inbox and outbox objects are probably at an excessive 
level of detail not appropriate for the rather abstract Part 2. And the 
proposals in 5.2 are rather difficult to read and understand, in comparison with the 
existing text of Part 2.


Finally, the authors' apparent skepticism with respect to RM-ODP is not 
justified. Their statement that "[w]hile it may be claimed that the RM-ODP 
Foundations standard is understood and used by its intended audience (the creators of 
specification languages, etc), we wonder whether this is the case" does not 
seem to be correct at least for two reasons. Firstly, the intended audience of 
the standard is substantially wider than that since includes specifiers who 
need to rely on a well-defined system of concepts to create specifications of 
business and IT systems in such a manner that these specifications would be 
readable and understandable by the appropriate project stakeholders. Secondly, as 
described in literature, the (informal but rather rigorous) definitions of the 
system of concepts provided by Part 2 of RM-ODP (while indeed having some 
defects) have been successfully used in many projects for this purpose. And the 
"UML community" -- whatever this term refers to -- has largely ignored RM-ODP 
foundations mostly for non-technical reasons the elucidation of which may be an 
excellent topic for another paper.
