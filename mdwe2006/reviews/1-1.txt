4) JUSTIFICATION AND GENERAL COMMENTS:


This paper addresses an interesting topic and the approach appears to have
significant merit. The paper is, however, let down significantly by a rather
poor overall rationale for the work and very unclear explanations of a
number of aspects and untested or unstated assumptions in many areas. For
example:


- In the introduction it is asserted that aural applications should be
"mainly (if not exclusively) aural rather than visual" - yet this is not at
all justified. Surely many existing 'visual' applications would benefit from
extension to incorporate 'aurality', but need not be 'mainly' aural. For
example, surely a conventional application which aims to also (but not
predominantly) support visually impaired users could be extended with aural
supoprt.


- In the related work introduction, the authors state that "aural access ...
Requires adapting web contents'. It is not at all clear why *adaptation* is
always required? In many cases it may be appropriate to natively design the
aural application directly, rather than 'adapting' other content. If the
authors are talking about a design method specifically for adapting existing
non-aural applications then this shouls be made clear.


- SALT is described, but there is no description of what it fails to deliver
that the authors of AURA are aiming to address.


- In section 3, the authors identify a set of design issues to be taken into
account - but there is no explanation of where these have come from or why
they are indeed appropriate issues to underpin the design approach. This is
rather important, given that these form the basis for arguing the
appropriateness of the approach. Are these issues simply of the "but
everyone knows these" type, or the authors anecdotally- or
experience-supported views? Or is there some research which supports an
argument that these are key requirements?


- At the beginning of the conclusion,the authors state "we have analyzed
important design issues". This would have been useful if indeed it had been
done - but I couldn't find in the paper anywhere any *analysis* of design
issues! They were simply stated and then used in supporting the proposed
approach.


- Whilst the paper includes an example of the application of the approach,
there is no effective evaluation, or at least a rigourous analysis of
whether it has addressed a set of identified problems (see the following
point, which relates to this). 


Possibly most crucially, at the end of the section on related work the
authors consider the differences between their work and other approaches -
but they do not explain at all why these differences are important or what
the problems with existing approaches are that they are explicitly trying to
address. Because of this lack of a clear set of problems with existing
approaches that are being addressed, it becomes very difficult to evaluate
the proposed approach, and to see if it has indeed addressed those problems.
In the absence of this, the proposed approach appears as just another
alternative, rather than a clear step forward which addresses an existing
problem. By clearly articulating the gap which this approach addresses, the
authors would greatly strengthen the paper.



Overall, the approach may well be very useful and represent an important
contribution to the field. Basiclly the paper presents a (possibly important
and innovative) solution, but without the problem being clearly identified
or analysed, without an analysis of the factors which affect the solution,
and without a clear evaluation as to whether that problem has actually been
addressed. I would strongly encourage the authors to continue with this very
interesting work, but to position it more carefully.
