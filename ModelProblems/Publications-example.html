Design considerations and requirements

Consider a portal for tracking bibliographical references. Such references are compounds that normally include the title, authors, year, and publication means (e.g. journal, conference, book, internal report, etc). Distinct Web sites offer great help to researchers to locate, relate, track or retrieve documents based on this notion. The most popular sites include:

  • The Scientific Literature Digital LibraryWeb site (www.citeseer.org) aims at cataloguing all the on-line available research documents in the computer science area, providing search facilities over the catalogue. Its services include a keyword-based retrieval of references. Once located, the corresponding document can be downloaded.
  • The Digital Bibliography&Library Project (DBLP, www.informatik.uni-trier.de/~ley/db/) web site offers to researchers mechanisms for retrieving the list of publications of a given author, or the list of publications whose title matches the provided keywords.
  • The ACM portal (portal.acm.org) is an example of a publisher portal that allows locating references based on the name and issue of a certain journal. Commonly, an abstract is also rendered, and the document can be retrieved in a subscription basis.

When looking for references, it is quite common for researchers to move along these distinct sites. For instance, a user can first have a look at the last issue of ACM TOIS, come across an interesting paper, and then move to DBLP’s site to ascertain whether its authors have published something else in a related topic. Once, these additional publications have been identified, the researcher can move to CITESEER to attempt to obtain these documents electronically. Unfortunately, the flow of reference data among the distinct sites is not automated, but manually achieved by the user (e.g. by cutting&pasting among distinct windows). This hinders productivity, it is error-prone, and prevents the definition of the most routine patterns of access. Even if the distinct sites were accessed from the very same page, they would still be isolated units, with the data and control flow manually achieved by the individual user. The real breakthrough in improving the user experience comes from “binding” these distinct services together by defining appropriate control and data flow dependencies. This is the aim of this portal.
